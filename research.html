Brain Aneurysm Foundation

They have funded nearly $5.5 million to date towards brain aneurysm research in the US and Canada.
They focus their research on making early detection better and advancing treatment methods further. Their goal is for better outcomes for their patients.
Their goal is to be able to identify aneurysms before they rupture, hopefully preventing many fatal strokes down the line.

Types of strokes

There are two main types of strokes, ischemic and hemorrhagic.
An ischemic stroke occurs due to a blocked blood vessel, either from a clot or the buildup of a fatty substance called plaque. These are the most common types of strokes seen.
A hemorrhagic strokes are a result of a blood vessel bursting or leaking in the brain.


FAQs

1. What does it look like when someone is having a stroke?

Here are some main signs you can look for using the acronym BEFAST:


    B: Balance - Sudden trouble or loss of balance or corrdination

    E: Eyes - Sudden blurred or double vision or a loss of vision in one or both eyes without pain

    F: Face Drooping - One side of the face is dropping or numb when smiling

    A: Arm Weakness - One arm is weak or numb. May drift downwards when arm is raised.

    S: Speech Difficulty - Slurred speech, unable to speak, or difficulty saying simple phrases.

    T: Time to call 911 - If a person you know shows any of these symptoms, call 911 immediatly.
    Even if any of the symptoms go away, they still could be having a stroke or have already had one.


2. Does it matter what age you are?

A persons risk of having a stroke does increase with age, however it can happen at any time.


3. What are conditions that can increase the likleyhood of having a stroke?

Previous conditions such as having a stroke in the past, high blood pressure, heart disease,
high cholesterol, diabeties, sickle cell disease, obesity, and having a family history of strokes
can all put someone at higher risk of having a stroke.


4. What can I do if someone I know is having a stroke?

If you notice someone showing any of the B.E.F.A.S.T. signs, call 911 immediatly. Also, take note
of when they first started showing symptoms and tell the EMT or nurse. Don't give them food, water, or
medicine. You don't know what kind of stroke they had and it could affect their ability to swallow.
